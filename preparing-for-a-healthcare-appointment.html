How to prepare for a GP or specialist appointment

A structured way to make short appointments more useful: what to bring, what to say, what to ask, and how to leave with a clearer plan.

Healthcare appointments work best when both sides can quickly understand the problem, the timeline and the decision that needs to be made. NICE describes shared decision making as a joint process between a healthcare professional and a person receiving care, using evidence alongside the person's preferences, beliefs and values. This guide helps you prepare for that conversation.

1. Before the appointment: turn the problem into a clear story

Start with one sentence: "I'm asking for help because..." Then add the timeline. Healthcare professionals often need to know when symptoms started, whether they are constant or come and go, whether they are improving or worsening, and what has changed compared with your normal.

Write down the practical impact. For example: missed work or school, sleep disruption, difficulty walking, eating, washing, concentrating, caring responsibilities, social withdrawal or repeated flare-ups. Impact helps explain why the problem matters even when symptoms are hard to measure.

2. Bring the information that changes decisions

  • Current medicines, doses, over-the-counter medicines, supplements and allergies.
  • Recent changes: new medicine, stopped medicine, infection, injury, pregnancy, surgery, travel or major stress.
  • Photos of visible symptoms if they come and go, such as rashes or swelling.
  • Previous results, clinic letters, discharge summaries or care plans.
  • A symptom diary if the pattern matters more than a single day.

3. During the appointment: ask for the decision, not just the label

Appointments are often about a next step rather than a final answer. Useful questions are: What are we trying to rule out? What is the working explanation? What should happen next? Do I need tests, monitoring, treatment review, referral or self-care advice? What changes would make this urgent?

If options are discussed, ask about benefits, downsides, alternatives and what matters to you. This fits the shared decision-making principle: decisions should take account of evidence and the person's values and preferences, not just a generic pathway.

4. If you feel dismissed or confused

Stay specific. Instead of saying "I'm worried", try "I'm worried because this is new for me, it is getting worse, and it is stopping me from working." If the plan is unclear, ask: "Can I check I have understood the plan?" Then repeat it back. If you expected a test or referral and it is not being offered, ask what would need to change for that to be reconsidered.

5. Before leaving: get the safety-net plan

A good appointment should leave you knowing what happens next. Ask when results should arrive, who will contact whom, how to chase if you hear nothing, when to book follow-up, and what symptoms should prompt GP, NHS 111, urgent treatment or emergency care.

Useful phrase: "If things get worse while I'm waiting, what should I do, and which service should I contact?"

6. After the appointment: write the plan while it is fresh

Make a short note of the date, who you spoke to, what was agreed, any medicine changes, tests requested, referral plan, expected timescales and warning signs. This helps if you need to speak to another service later or explain what has already happened.

7. A simple appointment note template

  • Main problem: one sentence.
  • Timeline: start date, pattern, getting better/worse.
  • Impact: sleep, work, school, caring, mobility, eating, mood.
  • Medicines/allergies: include recent changes and side effects.
  • What I need today: advice, review, test, referral, results explanation, medication question or safety-net plan.
