Choosing the right NHS service

A practical guide to choosing where to start in the UK when you are not sure whether to use 999, A&E, NHS 111, an urgent treatment centre, GP surgery, pharmacy or an existing specialist team.

This page is not a symptom checker and does not tell you what condition you have. It translates official UK service routes into a calmer decision process so you can choose the safest next step and prepare the right information.

Emergency first Urgent but unsure Routine and ongoing
Start with safety: if someone may be seriously unwell, life may be at risk, symptoms are rapidly worsening, or someone cannot stay safe, do not use this site to decide. Use 999 or A&E.

1. When the safest route is 999 or A&E

A&E is for serious injuries and life-threatening emergencies. That includes situations where waiting for an appointment or trying several services could be dangerous. Examples include severe breathing difficulty, signs of stroke, severe chest pain, collapse, seizure, heavy bleeding, serious injury, severe burns, overdose, or immediate mental health danger.

If you think A&E may be needed but you are not sure, NHS advice is to use NHS 111. If the situation feels immediately life-threatening, call 999. If going to A&E, take medicines with you where possible and tell staff about allergies, long-term conditions and recent changes.

2. When NHS 111 is the best starting point

NHS 111 is designed for people who think they need medical help right now but are not sure what to do. Depending on the answers given, 111 may direct someone to 999/A&E, an urgent treatment centre, out-of-hours GP, nurse callback, pharmacist, specialist urgent support, their own GP surgery or safe self-care.

Use 111 when symptoms feel urgent, when the GP is closed or unavailable, when the route is unclear, or when symptoms are getting worse but are not obviously life-threatening. Call 111 rather than using 111 online for children under 5, complex existing conditions, care plans or end-of-life care needs.

3. When an urgent treatment centre may fit

Urgent treatment centres are for urgent medical help that is not a life-threatening emergency. NHS examples include sprains and strains, suspected broken bones, cuts, stomach pain, vomiting and diarrhoea, rashes, high temperature and some mental health concerns. NHS 111 can help decide whether a UTC is suitable and may be able to arrange an appointment or arrival time.

4. When GP surgery is the better route

GP surgeries are usually the right starting point for ongoing symptoms, long-term condition reviews, non-emergency medicine reviews, fit notes, tests, referral conversations and follow-up. GP teams may include doctors, nurses, pharmacists, physiotherapists, mental health practitioners and care navigators, so the person you speak to may not always be a GP.

When contacting a GP surgery, be direct about urgency. Say when the problem started, whether it is getting worse, how it affects normal life, medicines and allergies, and what you are asking for: advice, review, test, fit note, referral, medication question or follow-up.

5. When pharmacy is a sensible first step

Pharmacists can help with many common minor illness and medicine questions. In England, Pharmacy First lets participating pharmacies provide advice and, where clinically appropriate under the service, treatment for several common conditions. A pharmacist can also advise when a GP, NHS 111 or urgent service is more appropriate.

6. When to use an existing specialist team

If someone already has a clinic, nurse helpline, care plan or specialist team for the current problem, check the latest clinic letter, patient portal or care plan first. Existing teams often give specific instructions for flares, results, medication monitoring and who to contact. If the team cannot be reached and help is needed now, use NHS 111 or emergency services depending on urgency.

7. What to prepare before contacting any service

  • One sentence describing the main problem.
  • When it started and whether it is improving, stable, worsening or recurring.
  • Severity, frequency, triggers and impact on sleep, work, school, eating, walking or usual activities.
  • Medicines, dose changes, missed doses, allergies and side effects.
  • Long-term conditions, pregnancy or recent birth, recent surgery, recent infections and relevant test results.
  • What you need from the service today: urgent advice, routine appointment, medicine advice, referral discussion, test result explanation or safety-net advice.
